## The Limit Statement

DFC's closed topological structure predicts that several classes of particles and
phenomena actively searched for in current experiments do not exist: axionic dark
matter, proton decay via gauge-mediated transitions, and supersymmetric partners
of Standard Model particles. These are not probabilistic statements. Each follows
from a structural feature of the substrate — a topological fact that holds at all
energy scales.

---

## Why Absence Predictions Matter for Engineering

In the Standard Model, the existence of the axion, proton decay, or supersymmetric
partners would open radically new engineering possibilities: axion-based detectors for
dark matter, new long-distance coherence phenomena from proton oscillation, SUSY-enabled
unification scenarios. Their absence defines equally significant constraints — it tells
experimenters what can only yield null results and tells engineers which technology
directions are topologically foreclosed.

An absence prediction from a fundamental theory is the strongest possible statement
a theory can make: not "we have not seen X yet," but "X cannot be seen, because the
required structure does not exist in the substrate."

## Prediction 1: No Axion — Strong CP Is Solved Geometrically

### DFC Account

In the Standard Model, the strong CP problem asks why the QCD vacuum angle θ̄ is
observed to be less than 10⁻¹⁰. The Peccei-Quinn (PQ) mechanism resolves this by
introducing a global U(1) symmetry whose spontaneous breaking produces a pseudoscalar
Goldstone boson — the axion — that dynamically relaxes θ̄ to zero.

In DFC, strong CP is solved by a different mechanism that requires no new symmetry
and no new particle. The D7 SU(3) closure occurs on the complex five-sphere S⁵ ⊂ ℂ³,
whose isometry group under the complex structure is SU(3). The CP isometry of S⁵ acts
by complex conjugation — a real isometry — which maps θ̄ → −θ̄. The D7 vacuum at
θ̄ = 0 is the unique CP-symmetric point; any departure costs energy set by the D5/D6
interface structure anchoring the CP-symmetric phase. The result is θ̄ = 0 from
substrate geometry rather than from a dynamical relaxation mechanism.

**DFC result:** θ̄ = 0 from S⁵ CP isometry [T2a, Cycle 147]. No PQ mechanism is
present in the substrate, so no axion can appear as a Goldstone boson of PQ breaking.

### Experimental Implication

No axion-like particle (ALP) detection experiment — ADMX, CASPEr, ABRACADABRA,
HAYSTAC, or any cavity-based or NMR-based axion haloscope — can return a positive
signal attributable to a QCD axion in DFC. The strong CP problem is not solved by
a new particle at any mass scale. Any apparent positive signal would indicate either
an experimental artifact or a different beyond-SM particle with axion-like couplings
that is not the QCD axion.

**Confidence:** T2a. The S⁵ CP isometry argument is structural and consistent with
all verified DFC results. The remaining T2a gap is the formal derivation of θ̄ = 0
from D7 dynamics rather than from the geometric symmetry argument alone.

## Prediction 2: Absolute Proton Stability

### DFC Account

In GUT (Grand Unified Theory) models, the proton decays because a single gauge group
unifies quarks and leptons — there exist gauge bosons that can convert a quark into a
lepton. The proton is only approximately stable because the X and Y bosons mediating
this conversion are very heavy.

In DFC, no such conversion is possible at any energy. The SU(3) color topology at D7
and the U(1) hypercharge structure at D5 are **topologically disconnected product
factors** of the substrate's closure topology. The D7 kink network that carries quark
color charges has no structural path to the D5 winding configurations that carry lepton
number. There are no gauge bosons in the DFC spectrum that connect these sectors, because
no such connection exists in the substrate's fold topology.

The proton lifetime is not merely very long — it is structurally infinite. No local
interaction at any energy scale can change the baryon number of a bound quark state
into a lepton.

**DFC result:** Proton is absolutely stable [T1]. The argument follows from the product
topology of D7 × D5 with no cross-sector coupling at any depth [Cycles 59–74].

### Experimental Implication

Proton decay experiments — Super-Kamiokande, Hyper-Kamiokande, DUNE, JUNO — can only
set lower bounds on the proton lifetime. In DFC, the true lifetime is infinite. The
current experimental lower bound τ(p → e⁺π⁰) > 1.6 × 10³⁴ years (Super-K) is
consistent with absolute stability and will continue to be pushed to higher values
without ever observing an event. This is among the strongest falsifiable predictions
in the model: a single confirmed proton decay event would definitively rule out the
DFC framework as currently formulated.

**Confidence:** T1. The product topology argument is algebraically exact given the
D7 = SU(3) and D5 = U(1) assignments, which themselves are T2a.

## Prediction 3: No Supersymmetric Partners

### DFC Account

Supersymmetry (SUSY) is a spacetime symmetry that doubles the particle spectrum:
each boson acquires a fermionic superpartner and vice versa. In DFC, particles are
topological defect configurations of the substrate — kinks, windings, and their
collective coordinate excitations. Spin-1/2 arises from Jackiw-Rebbi zero modes
at the D6/D7 interface (T2a, Cycles 217 and 235). Bosons arise from the kink's
translational zero mode.

The DFC spectrum is fixed by the substrate's topology: the particles that appear
are exactly those corresponding to the zero-mode structure of the Pöschl-Teller
potential with n = 2, plus the gauge structure from D5/D6/D7 Hopf closures. There
is no room in this structure for superpartner configurations, because supersymmetry
requires a relationship between the bosonic and fermionic sectors that is not
present in the substrate's V(φ) dynamics.

Specifically: the DFC potential V(φ) = −α/2 φ² + β/4 φ⁴ satisfies the Bogomolny
equation φ' = W'(φ), where W is the BPS superpotential. This looks superficially
like an N = 1 SUSY quantum mechanics structure. However, the "supercharge" that
would pair the kink with a fermionic superpartner does not produce a new physical
degree of freedom in 3 + 1 dimensions — it generates the Jackiw-Rebbi zero mode
already present in the spectrum. No new selectron, squark, or gluino-type particle
emerges from the substrate topology.

**DFC result:** No supersymmetric partner particles [T3]. The absence follows from
the absence of a mechanism that would produce them, not from an explicit proof of
non-existence. This is the weakest-tier absence prediction in this document.

### Experimental Implication

LHC Run 3 and Run 4 SUSY searches — stop pair production, gluino production,
electroweakino searches — will continue to yield null results in DFC. The LHC energy
frontier (√s = 14 TeV) is far below the Kaluza-Klein scale m_KK ≈ 1.4 × 10¹⁹ GeV;
any new physics at or below TeV scales in DFC must arise from the collective coordinate
sector (Higgs, W/Z from D6), not from SUSY multiplets.

**Confidence:** T3. The argument is structural — DFC has no mechanism for SUSY — but
not formally excluding. A proof would require showing that V(φ) dynamics in 3 + 1D
do not admit an extended superalgebra of the required type.

## Prediction 4: No Fundamental Magnetic Monopoles

### DFC Account

In GUT theories, magnetic monopoles arise as topological defects when a large gauge
group G breaks to the Standard Model subgroup H: π₂(G/H) = ℤ guarantees monopole
existence. The 't Hooft-Polyakov monopole mass is of order M_GUT/α_GUT.

In DFC, the closure topology at D7 produces center vortices — configurations associated
with π₁(SU(3)/Z₃) = Z₃ — but not monopoles. Monopoles require π₂(G/H) ≠ 0. The
DFC substrate does not have a symmetry-breaking pattern G → H with non-trivial
second homotopy group: the D7 SU(3) closure is a direct topological construction,
not a residual of a broken larger group. The Z₃ vortex structure fully accounts for
confinement without any monopole sector.

**DFC result:** No fundamental magnetic monopoles [T2a]. The Z₃ center structure
produces vortices, not monopoles, because π₂(SU(3)/Z₃) = 0 while π₁(SU(3)/Z₃) = Z₃.

### Experimental Implication

Monopole searches — MoEDAL at the LHC, IceCube, dedicated track detectors — can
only set flux upper limits. No GUT monopole will be detected because no GUT breaking
pattern is present in the DFC substrate. The center vortex structure that produces
confinement is a closed-loop configuration, not a point-like topological charge.

**Confidence:** T2a. The π₂ = 0 argument is topologically exact given D7 = SU(3).

## Open Questions

1. **θ̄ = 0 formal derivation.** The S⁵ CP isometry argument is structural (T2a).
   A formal proof from D7 dynamics — showing that the closure process selects
   θ̄ = 0 and not θ̄ = π — would upgrade this to T1. This requires the D7 closure
   dynamics not yet fully derived (Cycle 156–157).

2. **SUSY exclusion from V(φ).** The T3 rating for "no SUSY" reflects an absence
   of mechanism rather than a proof. A proof would show that the BPS structure of
   V(φ) in 3+1D does not extend to a full SUSY algebra.

3. **What DFC *does* predict for dark matter.** If no axion exists and the proton
   is absolutely stable, DFC must account for dark matter by another mechanism.
   Stable kink configurations at intermediate depth behaviors (D5/D6) are the
   leading candidate — but this has not been developed into a quantitative account.

4. **Proton lifetime and nucleon oscillation.** While τ_p = ∞ in DFC, bound-state
   quantum effects in the three-quark system could produce extremely rare
   configuration changes not involving quark-lepton conversion. Whether any such
   process exists in DFC at finite but large timescales is open.
