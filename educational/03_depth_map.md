## What the Depth Map Is

The substrate is one continuous object. As it compresses, it passes through a series of
reorganization thresholds — bifurcations. At each threshold, a qualitatively new pattern
of behavior appears. The **D-depth labels** (D1, D2, ..., D7) are names for these
compression thresholds.

Three cautions before reading the table:

1. **D-depths are not separate spaces.** The substrate does not split into layers. There is
   one substrate throughout. D3 behavior and D7 behavior both live on the same object.

2. **D-depth assignments are working hypotheses.** The correspondence D5 ↔ U(1), D6 ↔ SU(2),
   D7 ↔ SU(3) is supported by numerical predictions but has not been formally derived from
   V(φ) alone. These labels are provisional markers.

3. **Lower depths persist at higher depths.** When the substrate reaches D7 compression,
   D3 localization is still present. The behaviors accumulate; they do not replace each other.

## Why These Depths and Not Others

A natural question: why are there seven depths and not four or twelve?

**D1 through D4** reflect four types of behaviors that any physical description needs:
something that exists (D1), something that can propagate (D2), something that has a
position (D3), and something that resists motion (D4). The DFC claim is that these
emerge sequentially as compression increases — each requires the previous one.

**D5, D6, D7** are the closure depths, where the substrate forms topologically stable
loops at progressively higher winding complexity. The DFC working hypothesis is that
three and only three such stable closure configurations exist before the substrate
becomes self-confining at D7:

- D5: a simple circular loop (S¹ winding) — the smallest topologically non-trivial
  configuration. This corresponds to U(1) behavior — the simplest gauge structure.
- D6: a three-sphere wrapping (S³) — the next Hopf fiber. This corresponds to SU(2)
  behavior — the weak force structure with spin-1/2.
- D7: a five-sphere embedding (S⁵) — the third Hopf fiber. This corresponds to SU(3)
  behavior — the strong force structure with color confinement.

The sequence terminates at D7 because the D7 SU(3) closure is confining: the D7 substrate
behavior traps its own topological defects. There is no D8 because confinement prevents
further free closure configurations from propagating independently. This is a structural
argument (Tier 3); the formal proof is equivalent to the Yang-Mills mass gap problem.

## The Closure Topology in Plain Language

What does "S¹ winding" or "S³ wrapping" mean?

Think of the substrate field φ as a flexible cord. At D5 compression depths, the cord
has been compressed enough that it can form a closed loop. The simplest closed loop is a
circle — what mathematicians call S¹ (the 1-sphere, or simply a circle). When the substrate
closes into an S¹ shape, it produces U(1) behavior: the same behavior that underlies
electromagnetism and the phase rotation symmetry of quantum mechanics.

At D6, the substrate has compressed further. The closed configuration at D6 is more
complex: a three-dimensional sphere S³ (the surface of a four-dimensional ball). S³
has a richer winding structure than S¹, and this richer structure produces SU(2)
behavior — the non-Abelian weak force symmetry.

At D7, the closure topology is S⁵ (the surface of a six-dimensional ball). S⁵ produces
SU(3) behavior — the non-Abelian strong force symmetry with three-dimensional color
space.

The sequence S¹ → S³ → S⁵ is not arbitrary. These are the three Hopf spheres: the
only spheres that admit fiber bundle structures over lower-dimensional spheres. They
appear because the substrate's compression passes through the Hopf fiber sequence
naturally — each D5, D6, D7 closure configuration is the smallest available stable
winding at that compression depth. This correspondence is Tier 3 (Bottleneck 1 partially
closed in Cycles 59–74; the formal proof from V(φ) uses the complex extension of the
substrate field).

## D3 and D4: Before the Gauge Forces

D3 and D4 are preconditions for the gauge forces. They are less dramatic to describe but
essential to the structure.

**D3 localization** is what makes particles have positions. Before D3, excitations of
the substrate are fully delocalized — spread uniformly across the substrate's internal
organization. At D3 depth, the substrate develops localized excitation modes: kinks
and kink combinations that are concentrated rather than spread. From inside the system,
this concentration looks like being at a position. Three apparent spatial directions
(not one or four) emerge from the D3 localization behavior; the precise reason why three
rather than another number is connected to the D5-D7 closure topology sequence.

**D4 inertia** is what makes particles resist acceleration. Without localization (D3),
there is no "position to resist changing," so inertia has no meaning. With D3 in place,
the localized excitation at D4 acquires a stable effective mass — an energetic cost
to changing its localization state. This energetic cost is what appears as inertial mass.

The D3 → D4 → D5 ordering is therefore not arbitrary: localization before inertia before
gauge closure. This ordering is a structural claim of the DFC framework (Tier 3).

## What Is Still Open

The depth map is the most incomplete part of the DFC framework. Here is an honest list
of what is not yet derived:

1. **Why these specific depths?** The Hopf fiber argument explains why S¹, S³, S⁵
   appear in sequence, but a complete derivation starting from V(φ) and arriving at
   exactly this sequence — without assuming the Hopf structure — has not been completed.

2. **Why D7 terminates?** The structural argument (confinement prevents D8) is consistent
   but is equivalent to solving the Yang-Mills mass gap problem from DFC first principles.

3. **The D3 position operator.** The formal proof that D3 localization is the position
   operator in quantum mechanics (i.e., that D3 behavior reproduces the full Born rule
   for position, not just for spin) is open.

4. **Threshold positions.** The compression values α₅, α₆, α₇ at which D5, D6, D7
   closures occur — what specific compression thresholds trigger each closure — have not
   been derived from V(φ). They are currently read from the Standard Model's gauge boson
   mass scales, not from the substrate dynamics.

5. **D1 and D2.** These earliest depths are the least characterized. D2 propagation
   produces wave modes, but the precise relationship between D2 behavior and the massless
   spin-1 photon (after D5 closure) is not yet formally derived. D1 — the maximum
   compression state — is a necessary starting point in the DFC construction but has
   no direct observational signature beyond establishing the initial conditions.
