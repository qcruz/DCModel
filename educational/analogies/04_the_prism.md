## The Core Analogy

White light passing through a prism is not broken into its colors by the prism. The
prism reveals colors that were always present in the light but were not distinguishable
from one another. What looked like a single undifferentiated beam becomes three
identifiable bands — red, green, blue — that can be measured, compared, and used to
characterize everything downstream.

DFC's D3 localization behavior works the same way. The substrate, at and beyond D3
compression depth, produces three symmetric, equivalent directional components from
what was previously undifferentiated field behavior. What physicists call "spatial
coordinates" — the x, y, z by which everything is located — are not a grid that the
substrate lives inside. They are the three components that D3 localization reveals
in the substrate's own behavior, just as a prism reveals the components that were
always in the light.

## What Position Actually Is

The troubling question in physics is: what is a position? Not "where is this particle"
but what does "where" mean at all?

The standard answer — "position is a coordinate in a pre-existing three-dimensional
space" — shifts the question without answering it. What is the space? What determines
that it is three-dimensional and not two or four?

DFC gives a different answer: **position is a measure of how the substrate's local
field amplitude projects onto three components produced by D3 localization.** A particle
does not sit at a coordinate in space. A particle is a localized kink configuration,
and its "position" is the three numbers that describe how that configuration is oriented
within the three-component structure that D3 localization produces.

The prism analogy makes this concrete. After the prism, every light beam is described
by three numbers (R, G, B). These three numbers are not properties the light had before
the prism in any distinguishable way — they become distinguishable only because the
prism creates a structure in which each component can be measured independently.
Position, in DFC, is what happens when D3 localization plays the role of the prism.

## Why Three Components, Not Two or Four

This is where the prism analogy has an advantage over "space is just three-dimensional."
The prism has exactly three primary color components for a physical reason: the three
types of cone cells in the human eye define a three-dimensional color space in perception,
and the visible spectrum happens to be captured well by three primaries. The number
three has a physical explanation.

In DFC, the number three is also explained — but more rigorously. The compression cascade
proceeds in complex dimensions: S¹ (in ℂ¹), S³ (in ℂ²), S⁵ (in ℂ³). The cascade
terminates at the third step because the kink's shape integral I₄ = 4/3 exactly equals
the Casimir invariant of the fundamental representation of SU(n) only when n = 3.
Setting (n²−1)/(2n) = 4/3 gives a polynomial with discriminant 100 = 10², whose unique
positive integer solution is n = 3. The cascade must stop at three complex dimensions —
and three complex dimensions produce three real spatial directions.

The number three, in DFC, is not an assumption. It is a consequence of the mathematics
of V(φ) and rational arithmetic.

The prism analogy is honest here: it does not explain why three primaries. DFC's cascade
argument does explain why three spatial directions. This is where the analogy does real
work and where DFC adds something the analogy cannot.

## What the Analogy Captures

The prism analogy captures three things that are genuinely true in DFC:

**1. The components were latent, not created.** The light contained red, green, and
blue before the prism. The prism did not manufacture them. Similarly, the substrate
contains the three directional components before any observer is present to measure
them. D3 localization does not manufacture spatial directions; it makes them
distinguishable.

**2. All three components are equivalent.** No RGB primary color is "more real" or
"primary" among the three. The choice of which to call R, G, or B is a labeling
convention. Similarly, in DFC, no spatial direction (x, y, z) is privileged. There
is no preferred direction in the substrate's local symmetry. The apparent equivalence
of x, y, z in physics — the rotational symmetry of space — follows from the symmetric
three-component structure that D3 localization produces.

**3. Characterization is automatic and total.** Once light passes the prism, it is
automatically characterized by all three components simultaneously. There is no object
that has red and green values but lacks a blue value. Similarly, any kink configuration
beyond D3 localization depth has all three position components simultaneously. There is
no particle with x and y coordinates but no z coordinate. Three-dimensional
characterization is not chosen; it is the structure D3 localization produces.

## Where the Prism Analogy Breaks Down

**The prism requires a pre-existing space.** A physical glass prism exists in three-
dimensional space. The light travels through it in space. The analogy thus assumes what
DFC is trying to explain. A glass prism cannot illustrate how three-dimensional space
arises in the first place — only how a pre-existing structure is made distinguishable.

DFC's claim is deeper: D3 localization does not occur inside three-dimensional space.
D3 localization is the process from which three-dimensional space is an emergent
description. There is no containing space for the substrate to exist in before this
process. The substrate is not three-dimensional until D3 localization occurs — after
which the three-component structure is what observers record as "three-dimensional
space."

The prism can only illustrate the output structure (three distinguishable components)
and the property that they were always latent. It cannot illustrate the absence of the
containing space beforehand. For that, the analogy must be understood as a model of the
output only, not a model of the process.

## Open Questions This Analogy Raises

The prism analogy makes a natural question precise: if position is a projection onto
three components, what is an object that has only some of the components — or none?
In color terms: a beam blocked by a filter has zero value in one component. In DFC
terms: a substrate configuration below D3 depth does not yet have spatial position.
It is not at any location. This is the DFC account of why there are no "sub-D3 particles
at specific locations" — position, as a concept, does not apply below D3 depth.

The formal question: does D3 localization produce exactly three real spatial dimensions,
or does the three-component structure of D3 relate to the three closure depths D5, D6,
D7 in a more detailed way? This is open at Tier 3 — the structural argument produces
three, but the precise relationship between the spatial three-component structure and
the D5/D6/D7 closure depths is not yet formally derived.
