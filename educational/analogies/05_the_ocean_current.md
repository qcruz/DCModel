## The Analogy

Imagine currents spanning the full width of an ocean, each layer moving in its own
distinct pattern. As depth increases, pressure compresses the current. At a critical
depth, the current becomes so compressed that it tri-folds — connecting back to itself
in three distinct regions. Anything within or interacting with the current at that
depth can be identified by how much it is being affected by each of the three
tri-fold regions.

This is an image for D3 localization. The substrate at shallow compression depths
spans everything without a position structure. At D3 depth, the substrate's behavior
folds into a three-part closure. Anything at or beyond that depth acquires a
description in terms of how much it participates in each fold region — which is what
spatial coordinates measure.

---

## What This Analogy Captures

- Compression at a threshold depth causes a qualitative structural change (tri-folding),
  not a gradual shift.
- The three fold regions emerge from the compression dynamics — they are not imposed
  from outside.
- Position is a continuous participation measure across three symmetric regions, not
  a discrete label.
- Things at shallower depths (above the tri-fold threshold) simply don't have access
  to this three-part structure — they are not unlocated, they are in a regime where
  localization hasn't formed yet.
- The medium is still one continuous thing after the fold; the fold does not split it
  into separate objects.
