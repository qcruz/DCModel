## The Problem with "3D Space"

Most physics starts with space as a given. There are three directions — left-right,
forward-backward, up-down — and objects live at positions in this pre-existing arena.

The DFC framework does not start this way. The substrate is one continuous object. It does
not exist *inside* three-dimensional space; three apparent spatial degrees of freedom are
a downstream consequence of how the substrate organizes itself.

This is a harder claim than it might appear. It is easy to say "space emerges." It is
much harder to show *which specific behavior* of the substrate produces the particular
appearance of three spatial directions with their familiar geometry.

Module 02 describes the DFC account of how space appears — and, crucially, what the
evidence for this account actually is.

---

## What Does "Compression" Mean?

In Module 01, the word "compression" was introduced. The substrate drives itself toward
a more compact configuration. But what does "compact" mean for a field that does not
live in pre-existing space?

Here is the precise meaning: the substrate has an internal organization. At any moment,
the field φ takes different values at different "points" — but these are not points in
a pre-given space; they are internal states of the field itself. The substrate's tendency
to equalize, to reduce variation across its internal states, is what compression means.

Think of a long flexible rope held at both ends, allowed to go slack. Left alone, the rope
sags toward its lowest-energy configuration. The substrate's compression is analogous:
it tends toward a configuration that is, in some internal sense, more uniform. Kinks
resist this tendency — they are the topological structures that cannot be smoothed away.

The compression is not instantaneous. It proceeds through stages. As the substrate
compresses, it passes through thresholds where the existing topology cannot sustain the
increasing compression without reorganizing. Each reorganization is a **bifurcation**.

## The D-Depth Markers

The DFC working map assigns letter labels to the behaviors that emerge at different
compression thresholds. These are called **D-depths** — D for "depth" of compression.

Important: D-depths are not separate spaces, not distinct objects, not layers like an
onion. They are **markers for behaviors observed on the same substrate at different
stages of compression**. The substrate is one thing throughout. What changes is the
topological organization it has reached.

The current working map:

| Depth marker | What emerges | Familiar appearance |
|---|---|---|
| D1 | Maximum compression — undifferentiated | Precursor state |
| D2 | First propagation behavior | Wave modes |
| D3 | Localization behavior | Apparent position |
| D4 | Inertia behavior | Apparent mass |
| D5 | U(1)-type closure | Electromagnetism |
| D6 | SU(2)-type closure | Weak force, spin |
| D7 | SU(3)-type closure | Strong force, color |

Each row represents a compression stage where a new topological configuration becomes
stable. The substrate does not "pass through" these depths and leave them behind —
lower depths remain part of the substrate's structure at every later stage. D3
localization is still present when D7 SU(3) closure has occurred.

## Where Space Comes From: The D3 Account

The DFC account of apparent spatial position is centered on **D3 localization**.

At D3 compression depths, the substrate develops a qualitatively new behavior: field
excitations (kinks and their combinations) become localized — concentrated in a region
of the substrate's internal organization rather than spread uniformly. A localized
excitation has a definite internal position; two localized excitations can be at the
same internal position or different ones.

This internal localization is what appears as spatial position when observed from
within the system. An observer made of substrate excitations will find that some
excitations interact strongly (same internal position) and others weakly (different
internal positions). The pattern of these interactions looks, from the inside, like
objects located in a three-dimensional space.

Why three spatial dimensions rather than one or four? The DFC account: the number of
apparent spatial degrees of freedom is set by the number of independent localization
directions the substrate supports at D3. This is connected to the winding topology
available at D3, and to the sequence of closure events that follow (D5, D6, D7).

The three-generation count (Module 01) and the three gauge force depths (D5, D6, D7)
both point to three as the natural outcome of the DFC compression sequence. This
correspondence is currently Tier 3 — structurally consistent but not yet a formal
derivation.

## The D-Depth Ladder: Why This Order?

The specific sequence D3 → D4 → D5 → D6 → D7 is not arbitrary. Each depth
requires the previous one. Here is why:

**D3 before D4:** You cannot have inertia — resistance to change of motion — without
first having localization. A non-localized excitation has no definite position to
"resist changing." Inertia (D4) requires the localization behavior of D3 as its
precondition.

**D4 before D5:** The U(1) closure at D5 is a winding configuration of the field.
A winding exists around a topological object. Topological objects require a substrate
that has already developed inertia (D4) — an effective mass that makes the object
stable. Without D4, the kink dissolves; without a stable kink, there is nothing to
wind around.

**D5, D6, D7 in sequence:** The closure topologies at each depth (S¹ winding for D5,
S³ wrapping for D6, S⁵ embedding for D7) require progressively more compressed field
configurations. Each builds on the closure at the previous depth.

This ordered structure is the DFC claim about *why* there are gauge forces at all: they
are the natural output of compression reaching successive closure thresholds.

## Localization and Measurement

A central question in quantum mechanics is: what determines where a particle is found
when you measure it?

The DFC structural account: the measurement process is an interaction between two
substrate excitations — the particle being measured and the measuring device (which is
also built from substrate excitations). The interaction occurs at a specific D3
localization "point." Before the interaction, the particle excitation is spread across
multiple localization states. The interaction forces a definite localization.

This is not yet a derived result — the formal account of measurement in DFC is an open
research direction. But the structural framing is different from the standard quantum
mechanical one: the "collapse" of the wave function is the substrate choosing a
localization configuration when two excitations interact, not an abstract projection
imposed from outside.

## What Is Verified

The D-depth assignments and the emergence of apparent spatial degrees of freedom are
currently Tier 3: structurally consistent with observation, not yet formally derived
from V(φ) alone. The verified results that provide evidence for this picture are
downstream consequences:

- **The common gauge coupling** (0.006% match, Tier 2a) follows from the kink structure
  at D5. This is the strongest evidence that D5 correctly corresponds to U(1) behavior.
- **The Weinberg angle** (0.01% match, Tier 2a) follows from the D5-D6 ratio of
  winding configurations.
- **The strong coupling** (0.006% match, Tier 2a via ECCC) requires the D7 closure
  to occur at the right compression scale.
- **The three-generation count** (Tier 3) follows from D6 topology.

Each of these predictions comes from the spatial structure that D3-D7 are supposed to
produce. Their accuracy is the indirect evidence that the D-depth ladder is correct.

## What Is Still Open

Three questions about the spatial emergence account are not yet resolved:

1. **Why specifically three apparent spatial directions?** The DFC structural picture is
   consistent with three, but a formal derivation from V(φ) showing that D3
   localization produces exactly three independent directions is not yet in hand.

2. **The position operator.** In quantum mechanics, position is an operator with a
   specific probability interpretation. In DFC, the analog is the D3 localization
   structure. The formal mapping between these — showing that D3 localization *is*
   the position operator — has not been proved.

3. **The emergence of Lorentz symmetry.** The substrate has an internal organization
   that, when D3 localization appears, looks like three-dimensional space. For
   consistency with special relativity, this apparent space must combine with the D2
   propagation behavior to give four-dimensional spacetime with Lorentz symmetry. The
   DFC account of why Lorentz symmetry is automatic (not an additional postulate) is
   an open derivation.
