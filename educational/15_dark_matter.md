# Module 15 — Dark Matter: What DFC Predicts

**Audience:** This module assumes you have read Module 03 (the depth map) and Module 13
(mass from compression). No mathematics beyond multiplication and exponents is required.

**Status note:** The structural account of dark matter — as a closure that acquires
inertia at D4 but not electromagnetic charge at D5 — is T2a (consistent with all
verified DFC results). The mass estimate (~35 keV) is T3 (order-of-magnitude structural
argument based on depth extrapolation). The cosmological abundance Ω_DM ≈ 0.265 is not
yet derived from DFC parameters — this is the most significant open problem in the DFC
dark matter account.

---

## The Observation

Something in the universe exerts gravitational pull but does not emit, absorb, or
scatter light. We know it is there from three independent lines of evidence:

**Galaxy rotation curves.** The outer edges of galaxies rotate faster than they should
if only the visible stars and gas were present. The rotation speed should fall off with
distance from the center — it does not. The only explanation consistent with Newtonian
gravity is that there is additional mass spread throughout a roughly spherical halo
surrounding the visible disk.

**Gravitational lensing.** Light from distant galaxies is bent as it passes through
regions of space that appear optically empty. The bending corresponds to the
gravitational field of an enormous amount of invisible mass. Maps built from this
bending show a cosmic web of invisible structure that ordinary matter traces only
approximately.

**The cosmic microwave background.** The pattern of temperature fluctuations left by
the early universe encodes the relative amounts of ordinary matter and invisible matter
present when the universe was roughly 380,000 years old. The data require that
approximately five times as much invisible matter as ordinary matter was present —
a ratio that has been independently confirmed by dozens of experiments.

This invisible component — dark matter — interacts gravitationally and perhaps weakly,
but has never been detected directly. Its identity remains the most significant
unresolved question in observational cosmology.

## What DFC Says About Dark Matter

In DFC, every particle is a stable kink configuration of the substrate that has
anchored to a particular compression depth. The depth determines which properties
the configuration acquires. Recall from Module 03 and Module 13:

- **D4** — the depth at which resistance to change in localization appears. A closure
  that has anchored to D4 or deeper has **inertia**, and therefore mass.
- **D5** — the depth at which U(1) electromagnetic winding closes. A closure that has
  anchored through D5 acquires **electric charge**.

Every ordinary charged particle — electrons, quarks — has anchored to D5 or deeper.
Every photon has anchored only to D2 (propagation mode) without acquiring the D4
inertia that would give it mass.

Dark matter, in the DFC account, is a third category: a closure that **anchors through
D4 but not through D5**. It has completed the inertia depth behavior, so it has mass
and therefore gravitational coupling. But it has not completed the electromagnetic
winding, so it carries no electric charge. It cannot emit or absorb photons. It is
gravitationally active and electromagnetically invisible — which is exactly what dark
matter is observed to be.

This is not a new ingredient added to the model. It is a structural prediction: if the
substrate can form stable closures at different depths, and if there is a depth between
D4 (inertia) and D5 (charge) where a closure can stabilize, then dark matter is a
natural consequence of the same compression dynamics that produces ordinary matter.

## Why This Makes Dark Matter Dark

The electromagnetic force operates through D5 U(1) winding. A closure that has not
completed D5 carries no U(1) winding number — no electromagnetic charge of any sign.
It cannot couple to photons because the photon is the quantum of D5 winding
oscillations. The coupling is zero not because of some suppression factor, but because
the charge quantum number itself is absent.

Similarly, the strong force operates through D7 SU(3) winding. A closure at D4–D4.5
has not reached D7 and carries no color charge. It cannot interact via gluon exchange.

The only interaction available to a D4-depth closure is through the compression gradient
field — the same gradient that manifests as gravity for all closures that have reached D3
localization. A dark matter kink, having completed D3 and D4, is localized and inertial.
It curves the local compression field. It attracts other localized closures through
the common gradient. It gravitates.

## Mass Estimate

The lepton masses in DFC follow a depth-to-mass scaling: each step in compression depth
multiplies the mass by a factor κ ≈ 5.33. The electron sits at depth d_e ≈ 5 (the D5
electromagnetic closure). Moving from depth 5 toward depth 4.5 — between D4 and D5 —
gives a mass suppression:

A dark matter closure at depth d ≈ 4.5 has mass approximately equal to the electron
mass multiplied by e to the power of κ times (d minus d_e).

```
m_DM ≈ m_e × exp(κ × (d_DM − d_e))
     ≈ 0.511 MeV × exp(5.33 × (4.5 − 5.0))
     ≈ 0.511 MeV × exp(−2.67)
     ≈ 35 keV
```

This is T3 — a structural order-of-magnitude estimate based on the depth scaling
observed for ordinary matter. The specific depth d_DM ≈ 4.5 is not yet derived from
V(φ); it is a plausible intermediate value.

The 35 keV estimate puts the DFC dark matter candidate in the **warm dark matter**
regime — lighter than a WIMP (which would be at the GeV–TeV scale) but heavier than
a neutrino (a few eV). This is important because:

- Warm dark matter erases structure on small scales (below roughly 1 kiloparsec for
  a 35 keV candidate), which qualitatively helps resolve the missing satellites problem
  and core-cusp tension that plague cold dark matter.
- The Lyman-alpha forest observations constrain warm dark matter to be heavier than
  about 3.5 keV. The DFC estimate of 35 keV satisfies this comfortably.

## Gravitational Coupling Without Electromagnetic Coupling

A common question: if dark matter interacts gravitationally, isn't gravity a force too?
Why does the D4 depth give gravity but not electromagnetism?

In DFC, gravity and electromagnetism are interaction behaviors at different compression
depths. Gravity appears as a consequence of the compression gradient field — the
tendency of the substrate to relieve local compression by redistributing into neighboring
configurations. Every closure that has acquired D3 localization and D4 inertia
participates in this gradient. Gravity is what it means to be localized and inertial.

Electromagnetism, by contrast, is a D5 phenomenon — it is the oscillation mode
associated with the U(1) winding at the first electromagnetic closure threshold.
A closure at D4 participates in the compression gradient (gravity) without participating
in the U(1) winding oscillations (electromagnetism). The two couplings arise at
different depths and are structurally independent.

The electron has both because it has anchored through D5 as well as D4. Dark matter
has only gravity because it has anchored through D4 but not D5.

## What DFC Does Not Yet Explain

The structural picture above is T2a — it is consistent and derivable from the model's
framework, but several important quantities are not yet derived:

**The cosmological abundance Ω_DM ≈ 0.265.** Observations show that dark matter
accounts for about 26.5% of the universe's total energy budget, roughly five times
the ordinary matter fraction. DFC has no current derivation of why the substrate
produced approximately this much dark matter during the universe's early history. The
production mechanism — how D4-depth closures were generated in the first moments after
the initial compression event — is an open problem (T4).

**The thermal relic problem.** If DFC dark matter were produced thermally in the early
universe (in equilibrium with ordinary matter), a 35 keV particle would overclose the
universe by a factor of roughly 3000. This is a genuine tension: the mass estimate and
the thermal relic density are inconsistent. The resolution probably involves
non-thermal production — the dark matter closures may have been generated by compression
dynamics at the QCD transition or another non-equilibrium event, not by thermalization
with the ordinary matter plasma. But the specific mechanism is not yet identified.

**The self-interaction cross-section.** How strongly do dark matter kinks scatter off
each other? From the Bullet Cluster observation, the self-interaction must be very small
— less than one square centimeter per gram. DFC predicts that D4-depth kink–kink
scattering is extremely short-range (the interaction range is roughly the kink width,
about 6 nanometers for a 35 keV particle), which is qualitatively consistent with the
Bullet Cluster constraint. But no calculation of this cross-section from the DFC
field equations has been done.

**Why D4 and not D4.5 exactly.** The depth d_DM ≈ 4.5 is a structural guess, not a
derivation. The actual stable depth for intermediate closures — if it exists — must
come from a potential barrier or topological obstruction in the D4→D5 transition.
Understanding this barrier would determine both the dark matter mass and whether a
single species or a spectrum of dark matter candidates exists.

## What Remains Open

The DFC dark matter account has a clear structural picture — incomplete-depth closures
that acquire mass but not charge — but two quantitative predictions are missing: the
abundance and the precise mass. These require deriving the D4→D5 transition dynamics
from V(φ) and understanding the production mechanism in the early substrate. Both
connect to the broader open problem of how the D-depth assignments arise from the
potential, which is the model's deepest remaining derivation gap.
