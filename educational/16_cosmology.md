## What Cosmology Needs to Explain

The observable universe has three large-scale structural features that any fundamental
theory must account for:

**The universe is expanding.** Edwin Hubble established in 1929 that distant galaxies are
moving away from us, and the farther they are, the faster they recede. The rate of this
recession is characterized by a number called the Hubble constant, H₀. Measuring H₀
precisely — and explaining its value — is one of the central goals of modern cosmology.

**The expansion is accelerating.** In 1998, observations of distant supernovae showed
that the expansion of the universe is not slowing down under gravity but speeding up. The
agent responsible is called dark energy — a form of energy density that appears to be
uniformly distributed throughout all of space and does not dilute as the universe grows.
The energy budget of the universe is approximately 5% ordinary matter, 26.5% dark matter,
and 68.5% dark energy.

**Structure grew from near-uniformity.** The early universe was almost perfectly smooth.
The tiny temperature fluctuations imprinted on the cosmic microwave background — the relic
radiation from when the universe was 380,000 years old — are the seeds from which galaxies,
clusters, and the cosmic web grew. The pattern of these fluctuations encodes the relative
amounts of ordinary matter, dark matter, and dark energy, and has been measured with
extraordinary precision.

The Hubble constant in DFC is determined by a relation that connects the apparent expansion
rate to the density parameters of the substrate. Specifically, the Hubble constant equals
one hundred times the square root of a combination of the matter density parameter and
the dark energy density parameter, with units of kilometers per second per megaparsec.

In symbols:

```
H₀ = 100 × √(Ω_m × h² + Ω_Λ × h²)

where Ω_m is the fraction of the critical density in matter (ordinary plus dark), Ω_Λ
is the fraction in dark energy, and h is a dimensionless ratio close to 0.67.

```

The observed value from the Planck satellite's CMB measurements is 67.40 km/s/Mpc.

## What Drives the Expansion

In DFC, the apparent expansion of the universe is not a mystery of "what caused the Big
Bang to push matter outward." It is the ongoing consequence of the substrate's localization
behavior at D3.

Localization centers — the stable configurations that appear as particles and large-scale
structures — naturally propagate apart over the substrate's compression time, because the
substrate has no mechanism that would hold them in fixed spatial relation. The rate at
which apparent separation grows is determined by the substrate's energy composition.

Matter (ordinary and dark) acts to slow the apparent expansion, because matter produces
local compression gradients (what we call gravity) that pull localization centers toward
each other. Dark energy acts to accelerate the expansion, because it represents a
baseline compression energy of the substrate that does not dilute as the substrate's
localization pattern spreads.

The interplay of these two effects — matter slowing the expansion and dark energy
accelerating it — produces the cosmic history that observations confirm: an early
epoch dominated by matter (slowing expansion), followed by the current epoch in which
dark energy dominates (accelerating expansion). The crossover happened roughly five
billion years ago.

## The Cosmological Constant Problem

One of the deepest open problems in physics — and in DFC — is explaining why dark energy
has the value it does.

The observed dark energy density is:

```
Λ_cosm ≈ 2.9 × 10⁻³⁵ kg/m³
```

This is an extraordinarily small number compared to what quantum field theory would naively
predict for the vacuum energy density. In standard quantum field theory, virtual particle
fluctuations contribute to the vacuum energy at a scale set by the highest energy cutoff of
the theory. If that cutoff is the Planck scale — the energy at which gravity becomes
comparable to the other forces — the predicted vacuum energy is roughly 10¹²² times larger
than what is observed.

This discrepancy between the predicted and observed vacuum energy is the cosmological
constant problem. It is one of the largest unsolved problems in fundamental physics.

In DFC, the relevant energy cutoff is set by the kink width ξ ≈ 0.87 l_Pl — the
Planck scale is indeed the natural UV cutoff of the model. This does not, by itself, solve
the problem. DFC has no current derivation of why Λ_cosm takes its observed value. This
remains T4 — not even a structural argument is available.

The cosmological constant problem is the deepest open problem in the DFC cosmological
account. Resolving it would require understanding why the substrate's baseline compression
energy is so much smaller than the Planck-scale energy density of its kink configurations.

## The Cosmic Microwave Background

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) is light released when the early universe cooled
enough for electrons and protons to combine into neutral hydrogen atoms, making the
universe transparent. This happened when the universe was about 380,000 years old. The
pattern of tiny temperature variations in the CMB encodes the state of the universe at
that moment — including the ratio of ordinary matter to dark matter, and the geometry of
apparent space.

In DFC, the CMB structure arises from the pattern of compression fluctuations in the
substrate at the D3 localization epoch. The acoustic oscillations visible in the CMB
power spectrum — the sequence of peaks and troughs in temperature variation versus
angular scale — correspond to oscillations of the substrate's localization pattern,
driven by the competition between the compression gradient (gravity) pulling matter
together and the radiation pressure pushing it apart.

The location of the first acoustic peak in the CMB power spectrum measures the angular
size of the sound horizon at recombination, which in turn measures the geometry of
apparent space. Planck measurements confirm that apparent space is geometrically flat
to high precision — a result consistent with the DFC picture in which three apparent
spatial degrees of freedom emerge from D3 localization without any preferred curvature.

DFC has a structural account of cosmic expansion that matches the Hubble constant to 0.2%
once the energy budget is given. Three significant gaps remain:

1. **The cosmological constant.** Why is Λ_cosm so small? DFC has no mechanism that

   an incomplete-depth closure with mass but no charge. But DFC does not yet explain
   why the ratio of dark matter to ordinary matter is approximately 5:1. This requires
   understanding the production mechanism for D4-depth closures in the early substrate.

3. **Baryogenesis.** The universe appears to contain more matter than antimatter. In
   DFC, matter and antimatter correspond to kinks and antikinks with opposite topological
   charge. Why the substrate produced an asymmetry between them in the early universe
   is an open question connected to the CP violation in the D7 closure and the dynamics
   of the initial compression event.
