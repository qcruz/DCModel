## What Is Mass, Exactly?

In everyday experience, mass is what makes things hard to push. A bowling ball is harder
to accelerate than a tennis ball — that resistance is called inertia, and the number that
measures it is mass. In physics, mass has a second meaning: it is the energy a particle
carries even when it is completely at rest (Einstein's E = mc²). The two meanings are
connected: a particle that carries rest energy resists being accelerated, so inertial
mass and rest energy are the same quantity measured in different units.

In the Standard Model, mass comes from the Higgs mechanism — particles interact with a
Higgs field that pervades all of space, and this interaction slows them down, giving them
effective mass. This account is accurate and useful, but it does not explain *why*
different particles have the masses they do. The electron's mass is 0.511 MeV; the muon's
is 105.7 MeV; the tau's is 1776.9 MeV. These three numbers are inputs, not outputs.

In DFC, mass is not an input. It is a consequence of how a kink configuration sits in
the compression depth of the substrate.
