# Phenomenon: Interference

## Observation

When particles — photons, electrons, neutrons, even whole molecules — are sent through
two narrow slits toward a detection screen, they do not arrive in two bands behind the
slits. They arrive in a pattern of many alternating bright and dark bands, exactly as if
waves were passing through both slits simultaneously and interfering with each other.

This pattern appears even when particles are sent one at a time, with no possibility of
one particle interfering with another. Each individual particle lands at a single point.
Over time, those points accumulate into the interference pattern.

Blocking one slit, or installing detectors to determine which slit each particle passes
through, destroys the pattern — the two-band distribution returns. The act of gaining
which-path information eliminates interference, regardless of whether the information
is read.

## Standard Explanation

In quantum mechanics, each particle is described by a wavefunction that propagates through
both slits simultaneously. The two parts of the wavefunction interfere constructively (bright
bands) and destructively (dark bands). When detected, the wavefunction collapses to a
definite location with probability given by the squared magnitude of the amplitude.

Which-path detection entangles the particle with the detector, destroying the coherence
between the two paths and eliminating the interference pattern.

The standard theory predicts this with precision. It does not explain why particles have
wavefunctions, why measurement destroys coherence, or why probability appears.

A detector records individual particle arrivals. Over time, the distribution of arrival
points builds up into the interference pattern. It appears as if each particle somehow
"knew" about both slits.

From this view, the most natural question is: which slit did the particle go through?
The answer — that the question does not apply — sounds evasive. But it is the correct
mechanical answer: the particle did not travel through either slit as a classical object.
It was a localized stabilization event in a field that already occupied the entire
apparatus.

The field cannot simply divide into two independent parts — continuity requires that
redistribution through each channel remain correlated. The collapse rate is global;

This forces the field into a configuration where:
- Strain orientations reinforce in some regions (constructive — bright bands)
- Strain orientations cancel in others (destructive — dark bands)

This is interference. No notion of "path choice" is involved. There is only global field
reconfiguration under constraint.

- Mechanical

- Independent of whether the which-path information is read, recorded, or observed
