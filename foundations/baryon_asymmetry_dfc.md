## The Problem

The observable universe contains approximately one billion photons for every baryon
(proton or neutron). This ratio — the baryon-to-photon number η ≈ 6.1×10⁻¹⁰ — is
measured from the cosmic microwave background and from Big Bang nucleosynthesis, and
both measurements agree. The problem is that the Standard Model cannot generate this
asymmetry from symmetric initial conditions. Three conditions, identified by Sakharov
in 1967, are necessary for any mechanism:

1. **Baryon number violation** — processes that change the net count of baryons.
2. **C and CP violation** — the laws of physics must distinguish matter from antimatter.
3. **Departure from thermal equilibrium** — at equilibrium, any asymmetry produced is
   washed out by the reverse process.

The Standard Model satisfies all three in principle but quantitatively falls short by
many orders of magnitude. The CKM CP violation is far too small, and the electroweak
phase transition is not strongly first-order for the observed Higgs mass. This is one
of the clearest observational failures of the Standard Model as a complete theory.

## What DFC Honestly Does Not Add

At current tier (T3), DFC does not improve on leptogenesis or electroweak baryogenesis
quantitatively. The structural argument for out-of-equilibrium is novel, but:

- Leptogenesis also produces out-of-equilibrium conditions (heavy neutrino decay) and
  can naturally achieve η ~ 10⁻¹⁰ with appropriate parameters.
- DFC provides no new CP phases beyond the CKM matrix — so the CKM CP-violation
  shortfall that afflicts standard electroweak baryogenesis also afflicts DFC unless
  J is shown to take a larger effective value in the D6/D7 context.
- The absence of B−L violation in DFC means any baryon asymmetry generated at D6/D7
  closure cannot be regenerated if washed out. This puts DFC closer to electroweak
  baryogenesis than to leptogenesis in its structural requirements.

The compression cascade as the source of out-of-equilibrium dynamics is the clearest
genuinely new element DFC contributes to this problem.
